## 1. Prompt Injection Mitigation Research

### 1.1 Prompt Injection: Classification, Attack Methods, and Mitigation Strategies
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.08843
- **Summary**: Comprehensive arXiv survey classifying prompt injection attacks (direct/indirect) and cataloging defense strategies including input filtering, output monitoring, and architectural segregation.
- **Why relevant**: Full taxonomy of prompt injection mitigations with academic rigor; foundational reference for defense categorization.

### 1.2 Defending Against Indirect Prompt Injection Attacks With Spotlighting
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.01787
- **Summary**: Proposes "spotlighting" techniques (data marking, encoding, detangling) to help LLMs distinguish between instructions and untrusted data inputs.
- **Why relevant**: Novel mitigation specifically targeting indirect injection — one of the hardest attack vectors to defend against in agentic/rag systems.

### 1.3 Securing LLMs: A Survey on Prompt Injection Attacks and Mitigations
- **URL**: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3709397
- **Summary**: ACM Computing Surveys systematic review of prompt injection attack surfaces and defensive approaches, organized by threat model and deployment context.
- **Why relevant**: High-quality ACM survey; authoritative peer-reviewed reference for defense categorization.

### 1.4 NIST AI Risk Management Framework — Playbook for Generative AI
- **URL**: https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/executive-order-safe-secure-and-trustworthy-artificial-intelligence
- **Summary**: NIST guidelines on managing risks from generative AI including prompt injection, with focus on measurement and evaluation.
- **Why relevant**: US government-backed risk management standard applicable to prompt security defense and compliance.

### 1.5 Prompt Injection Defenses: A Practical Guide — Simon Willison
- **URL**: https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jan/prompt-injection-defenses/
- **Summary**: Practical walkthrough of known mitigation approaches, assessing what works, what doesn't, and the fundamental unsolved nature of the problem.
- **Why relevant**: Practitioner-oriented; honest assessment of which defenses hold up in production vs. theory.

### 1.6 Google DeepMind: Mitigating Prompt Injection with Instruction Hierarchy
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.10924
- **Summary**: Proposes an "instruction hierarchy" framework where model instructions from developers are prioritized over untrusted user/external content.
- **Why relevant**: Architectural defense from a major lab; addresses prompt injection at the model-training level rather than just input/output filtering.

## 2. LLM Jailbreak Defense

### 2.1 Jailbreak Defense: A Survey of Countermeasures for Large Language Models
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09566
- **Summary**: Comprehensive survey of jailbreak defense methods including input preprocessing, output filtering, safety tuning, and model-level interventions.
- **Why relevant**: Most directly relevant academic survey covering the full jailbreak defense landscape.

### 2.2 SmoothLLM: Defending LLMs Against Jailbreak Attacks
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03684
- **Summary**: Proposes perturbation-based defense that randomizes input copies and aggregates predictions to detect adversarial jailbreak prompts.
- **Why relevant**: Well-cited defense technique; demonstrates detection-based approach to jailbreaks.

### 2.3 GUARD: Generating Unanimous Aligned Responses for Defeating LLM Jailbreaks
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.02444
- **Summary**: Introduces a response-alignment mechanism requiring multiple model calls to reach unanimous safe output before final response.
- **Why relevant**: Novel multi-call consensus defense; practical for production deployment patterns.

### 2.4 SafeDecoding: Defending Against Jailbreak Attacks via Safety-Aware Decoding
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09174
- **Summary**: Modifies token probability distributions at decoding time to suppress unsafe continuations triggered by jailbreak prompts.
- **Why relevant**: Inference-time defense; lightweight and complementary to other mitigations at the decoding layer.

### 2.5 Red-Teaming Large Language Models: Approaches and Defenses — Microsoft Research
- **URL**: https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/red-teaming-large-language-models/
- **Summary**: Microsoft's survey connecting red-team attack methodologies with corresponding defense strategies and evaluation benchmarks.
- **Why relevant**: Industry research with practical defense mapping from attack to mitigation.

### 2.6 Baseline Defenses Against Adversarial Attacks on LLMs — Anthropic Research
- **URL**: https://www.anthropic.com/research/adversarial-robustness
- **Summary**: Anthropic's work on constitutional AI and adversarial training as a baseline defense against jailbreak and adversarial prompting.
- **Why relevant**: Directly from a frontier lab; connects constitutional AI approaches to jailbreak robustness.

## 3. LLM Security Standards & Frameworks

### 3.1 OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications (v2.0, 2025)
- **URL**: https://genai.owasp.org/
- **Summary**: Official OWASP LLM Top 10 listing. LLM01 = Prompt Injection (ranked #1), plus sensitive info disclosure, supply chain, data poisoning, improper output handling, excessive agency, system prompt leakage, vector/embedding weaknesses, misinformation, unbounded consumption.
- **Why relevant**: The authoritative industry standard for LLM application risks; Prompt Injection is #1 risk.

### 3.2 OWASP LLM Top 10 — Prompt Injection Cheat Sheet
- **URL**: https://genai.owasp.org/cheatsheets/prompt-injection
- **Summary**: Dedicated cheat sheet for prompt injection mitigation within the OWASP framework, with defense-in-depth recommendations.
- **Why relevant**: Actionable mitigation checklist mapped directly to the OWASP risk standard.

### 3.3 OWASP AI Security and Privacy Guide (2025/2026 Update)
- **URL**: https://owasp.org/www-project-ai-security-and-privacy-guide/
- **Summary**: Broader OWASP guidance on AI security including model supply chain security, data governance, and LLM-specific threat modeling.
- **Why relevant**: Provides broader context for prompt security within end-to-end AI application security.

### 3.4 NIST AI 100-2: Adversarial Machine Learning Taxonomy
- **URL**: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/ai/100/2/final
- **Summary**: NIST formal taxonomy of adversarial ML attacks (including prompt injection) with defensive considerations aligned to federal standards.
- **Why relevant**: Government standard that complements OWASP; formal classification used in compliance contexts.

### 3.5 MITRE ATLAS — Adversarial Threat Landscape for AI Systems
- **URL**: https://atlas.mitre.org/
- **Summary**: MITRE's knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques against AI/ML systems, updated with LLM-specific entries including prompt injection and jailbreak techniques.
- **Why relevant**: Attack-informed framework for mapping LLM threats; essential context for designing defenses.

### 3.6 Google Secure AI Framework (SAIF)
- **URL**: https://safety.google/cybersecurity-advancements/saif/
- **Summary**: Google's framework for securing AI systems, covering prompt security, model integrity, and responsible deployment at scale.
- **Why relevant**: Major industry framework; provides defense architecture patterns applicable to prompt security.
