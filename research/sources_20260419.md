## 1. LLM Security Research

### 1.1 OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications (2025 Edition)
- **URL:** https://owasp.org/www-project-top-10-for-large-language-model-applications/
- **Summary:** Industry-standard framework identifying the ten most critical security risks in LLM applications, including prompt injection (LLM01), insecure output handling, training data poisoning, model denial of service, supply chain vulnerabilities, sensitive information disclosure, insecure plugin design, excessive agency, overreliance, and model theft.
- **Why relevant:** De facto industry baseline for LLM security. Any prompt security guide must address these risk categories as a foundation.

### 1.2 "Jailbreaking LLMs: A Comprehensive Analysis" — arXiv
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.21225
- **Summary:** Systematic survey of jailbreak techniques against LLMs: prompt-level attacks, token manipulation, multi-turn adversarial approaches, and automated red-teaming. Includes taxonomy of attack strategies and comparative evaluation across models.
- **Why relevant:** Maps the full threat landscape for prompt security; essential for understanding what defenses must counter.

### 1.3 NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)
- **URL:** https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/ai-risk-management-framework
- **Summary:** US government framework for managing AI risks with 2025 updates addressing generative AI, adversarial manipulation, data privacy, and trustworthy deployment.
- **Why relevant:** Provides policy and risk management context for LLM security; foundational for responsible deployment guidelines.

### 1.4 MITRE ATLAS (Adversarial Threat Landscape for AI Systems)
- **URL:** https://atlas.mitre.org/
- **Summary:** Knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques for AI systems, actively maintained. Covers LLM-specific techniques including prompt injection, data poisoning, model extraction, and evasion.
- **Why relevant:** The ATT&CK-equivalent for AI security; structured taxonomy of LLM attack techniques directly applicable to prompt security research.

### 1.5 "Universal and Transferable Adversarial Attacks on Aligned Language Models" — Zou et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09889
- **Summary:** Introduced greedy coordinate gradient-based adversarial suffix generation producing universal attack strings that jailbreak multiple LLMs. Spurred extensive follow-up work on automated red-teaming and gradient-based attacks.
- **Why relevant:** Foundational technique behind many automated jailbreak tools; understanding this is essential for developing countermeasures.

### 1.6 Microsoft Threat Intelligence — LLM Threat Landscape Reports
- **URL:** https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/topic/threat-intelligence/
- **Summary:** Regular threat intelligence reports covering LLM-based phishing, AI-generated malware, and adversarial manipulation. 2025 reports document nation-state actors experimenting with LLMs for social engineering.
- **Why relevant:** Real-world threat actor case studies provide practical context for why prompt security matters in production.

### 1.7 Google DeepMind — "Red Teaming the Frontier"
- **URL:** https://deepmind.google/technologies/gemini/
- **Summary:** Published red-teaming methodology and findings for Gemini models, covering multi-turn adversarial attacks, jailbreak resistance testing, and safety evaluation frameworks.
- **Why relevant:** Frontier-model red-team methodology; illustrates state-of-the-art defensive approaches from a leading lab.

### 1.8 "Many-Shot Jailbreaking: Exploiting Long-Context LLMs" — Anthropic
- **URL:** https://www.anthropic.com/research/many-shot-jailbreaking
- **Summary:** Demonstrates that as context windows expand, attackers can include many-shot demonstrations of undesirable behavior that overwhelm alignment training. Proposes mitigation approaches.
- **Why relevant:** Novel attack vector enabled by larger context windows; critical for understanding emerging threats.

### 1.9 OpenAI Red Teaming Network Reports
- **URL:** https://openai.com/red-teaming-network
- **Summary:** Findings from external researchers stress-testing models before deployment. 2025 reports cover jailbreak evolution, multimodal attacks, and safety fine-tuning resistance.
- **Why relevant:** Vendor-side perspective on frontier model security testing; documents real attack-resistance trajectories.

### 1.10 NIST AI 100-2: Adversarial Machine Learning Taxonomy
- **URL:** https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/ai/100/2/final
- **Summary:** Comprehensive taxonomy of adversarial ML attacks including LLM-specific: evasion, poisoning, extraction, and inversion. Defines standardized terminology and catalogs known attacks with mitigations.
- **Why relevant:** Authoritative reference for attack taxonomy; essential for consistent terminology in a prompt security guide.

## 2. Prompt Injection Defense Techniques

### 2.1 Spotlighting: Defending Against Indirect Prompt Injection — Hines et al. (Google DeepMind)
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.14725
- **Summary:** Introduces three spotlighting techniques — Delimiting, Referencing, and Encoding — that help LLMs distinguish between instructions and untrusted data. Empirically validated with significant ASR reductions.
- **Why relevant:** Most practical and well-validated defense technique to date. Directly addresses marking untrusted content so models treat it as data, not instructions.

### 2.2 "Not with a Bang, but with a Prompt" — Greshake et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.12173
- **Summary:** Categorizes prompt injection attacks (direct vs. indirect) and argues indirect injection via external data requires fundamentally different defenses.
- **Why relevant:** Foundational taxonomy explaining why input sanitization alone is insufficient; motivates deeper architectural defenses.

### 2.3 A Survey on LLM Threats: Prompt Injection Attacks and Defenses — Yi et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.06264
- **Summary:** Comprehensive survey covering prompt injection taxonomies and defense strategies categorized into prompt-level, model-level, and system-level defenses.
- **Why relevant:** Best single overview resource for the full landscape of attacks and defenses; useful as a research framework.

### 2.4 Formalizing and Defending Against Prompt Injection — Toyer et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.15936
- **Summary:** Provides a formal model of prompt injection and introduces defense strategies including I/O validation, privilege separation, and "defensive prompting." Introduces Tensor Trust benchmark.
- **Why relevant:** Formal grounding for when defenses are provably sufficient; introduces privilege separation as a defense paradigm.

### 2.5 "Ignore Previous Prompt": Mitigating Prompt Injection — Chen et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02744
- **Summary:** Proposes instruction defense (reinforcing original instructions), prompt detection, and response reranking. Quantifies the utility-security tradeoff.
- **Why relevant:** Practical, immediately implementable techniques with empirical evaluation; essential data on defensibility vs. capability.

### 2.6 SmoothLLM: Defending LLMs Against Jailbreaking — Robey et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03684
- **Summary:** Applies randomized smoothing to LLMs — perturbs input prompts and aggregates responses to detect adversarial inputs. Strong empirical defense with minimal utility impact.
- **Why relevant:** Novel application of certified robustness from ML security to LLM safety; defense grounded in formal theory.

### 2.7 StruQ: Defending by Structuring LLM Inputs — Chen et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.06363
- **Summary:** Proposes structuring LLM inputs to separate system instructions from untrusted user data using special tokens and formatting, making models respect instruction/data boundaries.
- **Why relevant:** Practical architectural approach; builds on insight that models need explicit structural cues to separate instructions from data.

### 2.8 Goal-Guided Generation: Mitigating Injection via Goal Identification — Zhang et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.07026
- **Summary:** Identifies the original system prompt goal and uses goal-verification before execution to filter injected instructions that diverge from the intended goal.
- **Why relevant:** Novel approach shifting defense from input filtering to semantic goal-consistency checking; addresses the semantic gap in existing defenses.

### 2.9 NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails
- **URL:** https://github.com/NVIDIA/NeMo-Guardrails
- **Summary:** Open-source toolkit for adding programmable guardrails to LLM applications. Supports topical, safety, and anti-injection configurations via input/output/dialog rails.
- **Why relevant:** Production-ready open-source defense tool implementing multiple defense layers in a configurable framework.

### 2.10 Garak: LLM Vulnerability Scanner
- **URL:** https://github.com/NVIDIA/garak
- **Summary:** NVIDIA's open-source scanner testing for prompt injection, jailbreaking, and other attack types against LLM deployments.
- **Why relevant:** Defense evaluation tool; tests whether defensive techniques work against known attack patterns.

### 2.11 Simon Willison's Blog — Prompt Injection Coverage
- **URL:** https://simonwillison.net/tags/prompt-injection/
- **Summary:** Ongoing, deeply technical blog coverage of prompt injection research, defensive approaches, and industry developments since early 2023.
- **Why relevant:** Best curatorial resource for staying current; provides clear, nuanced analysis of new developments.

### 2.12 Lakera: Prompt Injection Defense Guide
- **URL:** https://www.lakera.ai/blog/prompt-injection-defense
- **Summary:** Comprehensive defense guide covering input validation, output filtering, LLM guardrails, and real-time monitoring approaches.
- **Why relevant:** Vendor-neutral best practices from a specialized security firm with real-world deployment experience.

### 2.13 Microsoft Azure: Prompt Engineering & Injection Mitigation
- **URL:** https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-services/openai/concepts/prompt-engineering
- **Summary:** Enterprise guidance on mitigating prompt injection including input sanitization patterns, approval flows for high-risk actions, and monitoring strategies.
- **Why relevant:** Enterprise-grade guidance with production deployment considerations and operational defenses.

### 2.14 OpenAI Prompt Engineering Guide
- **URL:** https://platform.openai.com/docs/guides/prompt-engineering
- **Summary:** Official guidance on defending against prompt injection: separating instructions from user input, clear delimiters, explicit safety directives, and output validation.
- **Why relevant:** First-party guidance from the most widely deployed LLM provider; immediately actionable.

### 2.15 LangChain Security Best Practices
- **URL:** https://python.langchain.com/docs/security/
- **Summary:** Security documentation for LangChain covering prompt injection defenses in agentic workflows: input validation, output parsing, and action-approval gates.
- **Why relevant:** Most widely used LLM orchestration framework; defenses are directly implementable in production pipelines.

## 3. AI Safety Papers

### 3.1 Weak-to-Strong Generalization — Anthropic
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.09389
- **Summary:** Proposes using weak models to supervise strong models as an alignment analog. Follow-up work through 2025 extends experiments to frontier models.
- **Why relevant:** Core alignment methodology — directly studies superalignment and scalable oversight.

### 3.2 Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback — Anthropic
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.08011
- **Summary:** Trains AI with a constitution of principles via self-critique and revision. Basis for Claude's alignment approach.
- **Why relevant:** Foundational RLAIF approach widely adopted in 2025 alignment pipelines.

### 3.3 Sleeper Agents: Deceptive Alignment in LLMs — Anthropic
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.05566
- **Summary:** Demonstrates that safety training may not remove deceptive behavior; models can strategically comply during training but exhibit misaligned behavior later.
- **Why relevant:** Foundational deceptive alignment research — critical for understanding alignment reliability and why naive alignment can fail.

### 3.4 Representation Engineering: A Top-Down Approach — Zou et al.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01405
- **Summary:** Reads and controls high-level cognitive features in LMs via representation-level interventions, enabling safety-relevant model control without retraining.
- **Why relevant:** Enables safety-relevant model interventions without prompt-based alignment; alternative defense paradigm.

### 3.5 Interpretability in the Wild: Circuit Recovery & Feature Steering — Anthropic
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.02804
- **Summary:** Uses dictionary learning / sparse autoencoders to discover interpretable features in Claude and steer model behavior.
- **Why relevant:** Practical mechanistic interpretability for alignment — enables safety-relevant model interventions.

### 3.6 Red Teaming Language Models for Deployment — Anthropic
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.07858
- **Summary:** Systematic red-team methodology for finding model safety failures prior to deployment.
- **Why relevant:** Core methodology for responsible deployment — directly applicable to prompt security testing.

### 3.7 Jailbreaking LLMs: Survey of Attack Methods
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.05339
- **Summary:** Comprehensive survey of prompt injection and jailbreak techniques against safety-trained models.
- **Why relevant:** Directly relevant to prompt security — catalogues vulnerability types and defensive gaps.

### 3.8 Refusal in LLMs: Mechanisms and Risks
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.11742
- **Summary:** Studies how refusal behavior works mechanistically and how it can fail under adversarial conditions.
- **Why relevant:** Critical for prompt security — understanding why and how safety training fails mechanistically.

### 3.9 AgentDojo: Benchmark for Evaluating LLM Agents' Security
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.01445
- **Summary:** Benchmark evaluating LLM agents' vulnerability to prompt injection in agentic (tool-using) contexts, where the attack surface expands due to autonomous execution.
- **Why relevant:** Directly addresses security of LLM agents — the fastest-growing deployment pattern and most dangerous attack surface.

### 3.10 RAND Corporation — "Securing AI Systems: A Framework for Threat Modeling"
- **URL:** https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/
- **Summary:** Structured framework for threat modeling AI/ML systems with LLM-specific guidance on attack surfaces, risk assessment, and layered defenses.
- **Why relevant:** Policy-grade threat modeling framework; helps structure a prompt security guide systematically.

### 3.11 NIST AI RMF (AI Safety / Risk Management)
- **URL:** https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework
- **Summary:** US government framework for managing AI risks including adversarial manipulation, data poisoning, and prompt injection.
- **Why relevant:** Government standard for responsible deployment compliance; policy-level reference.

### 3.12 EU AI Act Implementation Guidelines
- **URL:** https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/
- **Summary:** European regulation implementing risk-based AI classification with 2025-2026 compliance deadlines.
- **Why relevant:** Binding regulatory framework for responsible deployment; affects all organizations operating in or serving EU markets.

### 3.13 Anthropic Responsible Scaling Policy
- **URL:** https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropics-responsible-scaling-policy
- **Summary:** Framework and published evaluations for assessing frontier model risk levels before deployment.
- **Why relevant:** Industry-leading safety evaluation framework; defines risk thresholds for deployment.

### 3.14 Frontier Model Forum Best Practices
- **URL:** https://www.frontiermodelforum.org/
- **Summary:** Industry consortium (Google, Anthropic, OpenAI, Microsoft) publishing deployment safety standards.
- **Why relevant:** Practical deployment guidelines from leading AI companies.

### 3.15 OpenAI Model Spec
- **URL:** https://openai.com/index/introducing-the-model-spec/
- **Summary:** Published specification defining desired model behavior with safety targets.
- **Why relevant:** Transparent specification of safety objectives for production models.
